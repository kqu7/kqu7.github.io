Kaggle Cassava Leaf Disease Image Classification Task (Spring 2021)
Individual Project
Cassava Leaf Disease Classification is a multiclass classification competition held at Kaggle (https://www.kaggle.com/c/cassava-leaf-disease-classification). As the second-largest provider of carbohydrates in Africa, cassava is a key food security crop grown by smallholder farmers. However, viral diseases are major sources of poor yields, and to diagnose diseases, farmers require the help of agricultural experts to visually inspect the plants, which is labor-intensive and time-consuming. The objective of this competition is to identify the common diseases with the help of data science and machine learning.
In this competition, the train dataset includes 21,367 labelled images of Cassava leaves with four categories of diseases or healthy Cassava leaves. The task is to classify each image into one of the five categories mentioned above.
In this project, I built an end-to-end machine learning pipeline and ensembled a 3-model classifier trained on a dataset of 30,000 images To combat the noise within the data, I applied CUTMIX, dataset augmentation, TTA, and various image transformations to reduce the noise within the data I trained Trained models that had 90% accuracy on the 5-fold validation and ranked 84/3947 (top 3%) in the private leader board

Evaluating the Impact of Highway Network Desing on Population Dynamics (Spring 2021)
Group members: Ge Zhang, Zhe Song, Kai Qu [All members were CSE students at Georgia Tech.]
In this project, we aim to detect the impacts of road and highway network development on population dynamics under different scenarios using three models (single-species model, predator vs prey model, and multi-species interactions model) implemented in python
For each model, we define the main behaviors of the population as growth and dispersal based on the reaction diffusion system, in which the dispersal can be considered as the emigration process by animal populations. Initially, we have three kinds of population distributions (uniform, clustered, and even distributions) to simulate the distributions of different species. Over generations of populations, we track the density of population and its growth, analyzing the sensitivity of different species on the locations of highways.
In conclusion, we observed that the construction site of highway roads has a significant impact on the population, killing three many times population when it is constructed in the center than when constructed on the peripheral sites. Also, by comparing the population change of different scenarios, we also discovered that the competitive relationship between species can result in a lower death rate caused by highroads.

Simulation of Biology (Spring 2021)
Individual Project
This is a course project for CS 7492 Simulation of Biology taken at Georgia Tech in Spring 2021. I implemented four classical models (game of life, SIR, Gray-Scott diffusion) that are frequently used in biological observation and analysis, using the programming language Processing.
For the game of life simulation, a cellular automata simulator is written. Users can control or create cell patterns by using mouse clicks and can also enter single-step or continuous mode by using keystrokes. In the SIR model of disease transmission, a grid and agent based SIR epidemic simulator was implemented. Furthermore, a subpopulation graph that tracked the change of different populations (infected, uninfected, recovered) was also made to analyze the trends over time. In the reaction-diffusion project, I simulated a reaction-diffusion system known as Gray-Scott and created patterns of spots, stripes or spiral waves by initializing the system with different parameter settings.

Exploration of Algorithms on Coping with the MVC Problem (Fall 2020)
Group members: Tiancheng Ye, Chenying Liu, Kai Qu [All members were CSE students at Georgia Tech.]
We implemented four different algorithms (branch and bound, approximation, and two variations of local search algorithms) attempting to cope with a well-known NP-hard problem in graph theory, the minimum vertex cover (MVC). We measured the performance of different algorithms in terms of running time and relative error, finding that one variation of the implemented local searches performed best in terms of the above two criterias but the performance depended significantly on the starting point of the search.
In addition, we analyzed the theoretical time and space complexities of the four algorithms, comparing the theoretical time complexities to the actual running time and providing reasons for some inconsistencies on certain graph instances.

Optimal Review Ranking for Improving Shopper’s Decision Making (Spring 2020)
Group members: Akhil Sai Peddireddy (ap3ub), Kai Qu (kq4ff), Rakshita Kaulgud Ramesh (rrk7pb), and Haoran Zhu (hz3fr) [All members were CS students at UVA.]
We developed a system that provides personalized ranking of customer reviews for each shooper. Using cumulative position-weighted score, we were able to demonstrate that there is an average of 20% increase in user satisfaction using our personalized ranking system compared to the default Amazon ranking system.
Also, we proposed a method of rating products at term level based on users’ preferences. For example, if a user cares very much about the price of a product, we’ll offer another rating of the product solely based on the price factor. This method can be applied to recommendation systems to provide accurate recommendations.
